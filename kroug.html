A Visit to the Church of Our Lady Of Kazan in Moisenay

In the spring of this year, 2026, I had the great privilege to go and attend liturgy and see the frescoes at the Church of Our Lady Of Kazan (Eglise de Notre Dame de Kazan) in Moisenay, France - an Eastern Orthodox church.

The church building itself was designed and built solely by Archimandrite Euthyme (Wendt) (+1973), constructed using stones from the surrounding farmland. The church was consecrated in 1964, and has celebrating the divine liturgy regularly ever since. Fr. Gregory Kroug (+1969), a monastic iconographer, painted the frescoes in the early 1960's. He is an incredibly important figure in iconography (associated with the 'rediscovery of the icon' that happened in the 20th century), and, painted towards the end of his life, many consider these frescoes to be some of his best work.

It's incredibly unique that such a high point in Church art has been reached so recently, usually when speaking about the 'height of iconography', discussion is focused almost entirely on icons and frescoes which were painted in or before the 16th century, before the western influence of the renaissance infiltrated the iconography/liturgical art of the eastern church, helping lead to its disorientation.

More information about the Church and it's history is available on the Church's website (in French): https://skite-notredame-de-kazan.blogspot.com/p/histoire-de-lermitage.html

It's worth noting that there is not a single right angle present anywhere in the design of the building.

Orthodox Icons

Icons are one of my life interests. It didn't feel correct to put them on the page of my favorite artwork, since they are not strictly artwork.

When first seeing icons, I was really surprised by how a lot of them employ a reversed perspective, even hundreds of years before linear perspective was 'invented' in Italy during the renaissance. A great essay to read, from Pavel Florensky's "Beyond Vision: Essays on the Perception of Art", is the chapter on Reverse Perspective: https://monoskop.org/images/1/11/Florensky_Pavel_1967_2002_Reverse_Perspective.pdf

Church of the Transfiguration of Our Savior on Ilyin Street, Veliky Novgorod

These frescoes are attributed to Theophanes the Greek (the teacher of Andrei Rublev), and were executed in 1378. They are the only surviving examples of Theophanes the Greek's frescoes, despite him painting over 40 churches during his life. These specific frescoes are highly unique for the restricted colors (lots of fire/red and sharp light to correspond with the fact that these frescoes are in a church consecrated and dedicated to the Transfiguration.

Fr. Gregory Kroug:

Born in 1908 in St. Petersburg, Russia, George Kroug emigrated first to Estonia, and then to Paris, attending art school there in 1931. During this time, he met Leonid Ouspensky, who was one of his classmates. Both soon turned their focus from secular artwork towards the icon. Kroug kept an interest and ties in the avant-garde art scene which existed in Paris at the time; unlike Ouspensky, who fully gave up all interest in secular work in favor of the icon. This was during the time that Kroug and Ouspensky were trying to create a "Western Orthodox" style of iconography - it's my personal opinion that Kroug's maintenance of these ties to secular art was not a hesitance to fully pursue the icon, but was partly a way to nourish his creation of icons. Matisse did this inversely, when he went to Russia in 1911; and was deeply shocked by icons, he took visual things from the exterior of them to use in his work. Kroug's interaction with secular work seems more like taking something back from contemporary painting. In some of his frescoes, we can see dark thick outlines and bright colors, similar to Matisse's way of painting. Fr. Gregory took back and transfigured these elements which Matisse (and other contemporary artists of the time) adapted from iconography to suit secular painting; and brought them back into iconography, and captive to Christ.

After much suffering, stress from trying to paint in a style unnatural to him (the 'Western Orthodox' style), the nazi occupation of Paris, George suffered a crisis that placed him in a mental asylum at the beginning of the 1940's. Heiromonk Sergei (Schevich) visited him daily, he began to recover, return to society, and shortly afterwards moved to Vanves where Fr. Sergei was founding a monastic skete. It was here, in 1945, that he became a novice, and 3 years later was tonsured into monastic life, recieving the name "Gregory" after Saint Gregory the iconographer of the Kiev Caves.

After Fr. Gregory reposed in 1969, his notes were compiled into a book, Carnets d'un peintre d'icônes. This is an *incredible* book; but unfortunately is only available in French. Each chapter goes over one of the main types of icons (e.g. certain feast days, etc) - and there is a large chapter on representations of God the Father. One of the chapters can be viewed online by clicking here. I have translated this book from French to English, and will update here if anything is possible regarding publishing, etc.

Leonid Ouspensky:
Unlike Fr. Gregory, Leonid Ouspensky wrote and published texts, and was also a teacher of iconography with many students.

As it's too long to include in this section; a more complete bio of Ouspensky can be found here: https://www.gsinai.com/leonid-ouspensky-biography

Ouspensky and Fr. Gregory Kroug met at art school in Paris, and became lifelong friends, often working together, even sometimes working together on the same icon. Ouspensky completely gave himself to iconography, most days working 16 hours, this allowed him to work with many different mediums (painting, fresco, wood, metal, embroidery) - as well as writing and teaching. Ouspensky reposed in 1987 at the age of 85.

Elizaveta Ozolina, a French icon painter, said: «Just before his death, you could say, a few days before, I visited him, and, he was barely able to speak, but he said something that still simply, how can I say it, shakes me. He said "Have we understood what an icon is?" After everything he wrote, after all his works, after his whole life, which he dedicated to the icon, this was, how can I tell you, proof of how humble he was.. And precisely humble. He still felt that perhaps he did not understand what an icon was. »

Ouspensky's books "The Meaning of Icons" (written with Vladimir Lossky), and "The Theology of the Icon" (a two volume set) are incredibly rich, and indispensable for anyone wanting to deepen their knowledge about icons, whether or not they are interested in making icons themselves. "The Meaning of Icons" is more accessible, and includes a lot of high quality images of historical icons. "The Theology of the Icon" is much more dense, and without as many pictures.

(https://editions-orthodoxes.fr/boutique/livres/histoire/theologie-en-couleur-les-fresques-des-fetes-en-la-cathedrale-des-trois-saints-hierarques-a-paris/) - the book is trilingually English, French, and Russian)

Ouspensky (in response to a student who had drawn the hands on an icon in a naturalistic manner):
"The hands, the nose, the ears are rather signs, do you see, they are types of outline, but they are used in such a manner that they belong as an integral part of the depicted person. Because these are sense organs they feel something other than natural matter, do you understand? Remember the transfiguration of saint Seraphim; his disciple asks him:

«What are you feeling? - I can smell a perfume that does not exist in this world, and I feel a warmth (it was in winter, wasn't it, in the forest, and both were covered with snow), a kind of warmth which is not that of wooden fire; an absolutely extraordinary kind of warmth»

So, do you see, that is the reason why in an iconographic drawing, sense organs are restricted to signs. In your drawing, the line seeks to imitate nature, and this is not only foreign to the icon, but it is the opposite of the icon. Look what you have done with these dashes, which are supposed to be the folds of the mantle; one does not understand anything any more: neither where it comes from, nor where it goes. In an icon, everything is logical, even the folds of the robe; they underline the shape of the body, they are not lines traced at random, no matter how and no matter where. (He shows an ancient icon undergoing restoration.) Look at this movement, it is extraordinary because it follows the natural movement of the body, but it is not naturalistic."

Saint Sophrony of Essex (+1993):
One of the monumental saints and one of the most noted ascetic monks of the 20th century, St. Sophrony of Essex, born Sergei Sakharov, has left behind numerous writings; perhaps most well known as the disciple and biographer of St. Silouan the Athonite; but also the founder of the Stavropegic Monastery of St. John the Baptist in Essex, England, and as an important iconographer of the 20th century.

St. Sophrony was raised Orthodox and spent much time in Church and in prayer from a young age. He signed up for art education at the end of his secondary education, and in 1918 applied to the Moscow School of Fine Arts (where one of his teachers was Kandinsky). World War 1 had begun and the Bolshevik Revolution followed soon after - Sergei was deeply affected by the sheer amount of human suffering he witnessed then, leading him to abandon Christianity;

“I can remember myself as I was then, behaving in everyday life like any of my contemporaries though there were moments when I could not feel the earth under my feet. I could see it with my eyes but in spirit I was moving over a bottomless abyss.” “I was living as if in a dream. And that dream was a nightmare…at times a dark abyss opened under me and a thick leaden wall was looming over me. I was twice arrested at the beginning of the revolution. Many people perished at the time but I felt no fear, as if there were nothing terrible in those arrests. But the vision of that wall and that abyss put me in a state of inexplicable, silent terror. And this went on for many years.”

He spent years like this, before a mystical experience occured on Great Friday, bringing him back to the Church. He tried to balance secular artwork and prayer (since he was an artist and living in Paris at the time) - but ultimately was unable to do so, since both required the entire person.

"The battle between art and prayer, that is, between two forms of life which require the whole of man, continued within me in a very intense manner for a year and a half or two years. In the end, I was convinced that the means at my disposal in art would not give me what I was looking for. That is, even if I could intensely sense eternity through art, this awareness is not as deep as prayer. And I decided to give up art–which for me was a terribly high price to pay."

In 1926, at the age of 30, he arrived at Mt. Athos, where he would meet his spiritual father St. Silouan the Athonite, and receive his monastic tonsure.

“An artist is a person who believes with deep conviction in the rightness of what he creates; who devotes his entire life to art for mankind. Only in such people does the Divine spark burn, brightly an unquenchably. And this is what’s most important in art.” - St. Sophrony of Essex

Mother Juliana (Sokolova)
Mother Juliana (born in 1899 as Maria Sokolova) is perhaps the most important figure when discussing iconography in Russia during the 20th century.

During a time of intense Soviet persecution, she took enormous risks to preserve the art of iconography - studying church art through ancient monuments, both artistically and theologically, and immersing herself deeply in the tradition, she was able to create new images/prototypes, spiritually close to the works found in the 14th-16th centuries, at the height of iconography.

During her life, she noticed a lack of understanding of ancient icons within the Church community - at a time when preference was given to painted images with renaissance influence. In response to this, she taught many students; technical instruction for iconographers, lectures on art, iconography, and the theology of the icon directed specifically for future priests, and she leaves behind to us today very important writing/teaching material. She also worked in restoring ancient icons. Unfortunately, not very much information about Mother Juliana exists in English - at the bottom of this page there are some links; some of them are in Russian and can be read with google translate. We are fortunate enough that her work "The Iconographer's Labor", a wonderful instructional text, has been translated into English.

In 1970, she secretly took monastic vows and was tonsured in the name of the Holy Martyr Juliana. She reposed in 1981, after roughly 60 years of painting icons.
The two images above are frescoes made by Mother Juliana, in the Serapion Tent in the Monastery of St. Sergius. According to ancient monastery tradition, the wooden cell of St. Sergius stood on this site, so the theme of the murals is scenes of his life.
Above: The appearance of the Mother of God to St. Sergius of Radonezh, 1950
Above: St. Philaret
Above (from left to right): St Basil the Great, Christ Pantocrator, The Mother of God of the Sign.
Mother Juliana made various sketches in order to teach / to be used by other iconographers. The highlights are in reverse on Christ and St. Basil.
Above: The Venerable Miracle Workers of All Russia
Above: All Saints Who Shone Forth in the Russian Land
Above: St. Theodore Stratelates - an *exceptional* sketch; ID'd by one of my friends, who is an iconographer monk at Holy Cross Monastery.
Above: The Trinity. The last icon Mother Juliana made before her repose.

"Symbolically, already gravely ill, Nun Juliana painted an icon of the Holy Trinity. This work, which now hangs above the entrance to the Holy Gates of the Trinity Lavra of St. Sergius, was Nun Juliana's last. Nun Juliana's blessed death occurred on February 16, 1981. An hour before her death, she received the Holy Mysteries of Christ in full consciousness. The Lord graciously delivered her from the excruciating suffering inherent in her grave illness." (source)
